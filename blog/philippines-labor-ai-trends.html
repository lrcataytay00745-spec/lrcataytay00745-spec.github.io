Is AI Already Reshaping Philippine Jobs? What 20 Years of Labor Data Really Show

A data-driven look at employment, gender gaps, and job quality as the Philippines enters the generative-AI era.

lrcataytay00745-spec May 2026 ~10–12 min read

Dear readers,

Over the past few years, we have heard two competing stories about artificial intelligence and jobs in the Philippines. One says that AI will wipe out service work, especially in BPO and other knowledge-intensive sectors. The other says AI will be a productivity boost that creates new opportunities. To move beyond headlines, I decided to ask a simple question: what does the data actually show about Philippine employment as we enter the AI era?

To answer this, I used public Labor Force Survey data from the Philippine Statistics Authority, tracking national labor-market indicators from 2005 to 2025 and monthly patterns from 2021 to early 2026. I looked at four core indicators: labor force participation, employment, unemployment, and underemployment, broken down by sex and by broad sector. In short, I wanted to know who is working, in what kinds of jobs, and how that has changed over time.

The headline story is surprisingly calm: by the time generative AI takes off globally in the early 2020s, the Philippine labor market has largely recovered from COVID-19, unemployment is down, and employment is high. The real vulnerabilities lie elsewhere: persistent gender gaps in participation, a deep shift into services, and chronically high underemployment.

Data and how I structured the analysis

The backbone of this project is the Labor Force Survey, the standard source for employment statistics in the Philippines. I combined annual data from 2005 to 2025 with monthly series from 2021 to 2026, allowing me to see both long-run trajectories and high-frequency dynamics around the pandemic and the early AI window.

Core indicators tracked

Labor force participation rate: labor force as a share of the population aged 15 and over.

Employment rate: employed as a share of the labor force.

Unemployment rate: unemployed as a share of the labor force.

Underemployment rate: underemployed as a share of those who already have jobs.

I split the 2005–2025 period into three windows: a pre-pandemic baseline (2005–2018), the pandemic and early recovery (2019–2021), and a post-pandemic, early-AI window (2022–2025).

What 20 years of national labor data say

Participation has been stable, not collapsing

Between 2005 and 2025, the national labor force participation rate moves within a fairly tight corridor around the mid-60 percent range. There is a modest dip around the COVID-19 years, but the series quickly returns to its historical band. The AI era does not arrive after a collapse in participation; it arrives against a backdrop of long-standing, relatively flat engagement with the labor market.

Employment is high and unemployment has quietly improved

The employment rate has been consistently high for two decades, typically in the low- to mid-90 percent range. During major shocks, such as the global financial crisis and COVID-19, it dips toward 90 percent. After 2021, the employment rate recovers to around 95–96 percent, matching or slightly exceeding pre-pandemic levels.

The unemployment rate falls gradually from around 8 percent in the mid-2000s to roughly 4–5 percent in recent years, with a clear spike to about 10 percent at the height of COVID-19. Once the economy reopens, unemployment resumes its downward path. The 2022–2025 AI-era window looks like a continuation of that long-run improvement, not an outlier.

Underemployment remains the stubborn problem

Underemployment tells a less comfortable story. In the mid-2000s, around one in five employed workers reports wanting more hours or better work, with rates in the low-20 percent range. By the mid-2020s this has fallen to around 11–13 percent, but that still means underemployment is more than double the unemployment rate. For any AI discussion, this matters: the Philippines does not enter the AI era as a country without jobs, but as a country where many jobs are still not good enough.

How men and women experience the recovery and AI window

Gender gaps in participation are narrowing, but slowly

Men are consistently more likely to be in the labor force than women, but the gap is shrinking. Male labor force participation mostly sits between 72 and 77 percent. Female participation, which starts in the high-40s to low-50s in early 2021, rises toward the mid-50s by 2023–2026. Early in the period, gaps of 25–27 percentage points are common. Later, typical values imply gaps closer to 20–22 points. The AI era arrives in a labor market that is increasingly, but not yet equally, female.

Once in the labor force, men and women have similar employment chances

Conditional on being in the labor force, men and women face very similar employment probabilities, typically in the low- to mid-90s. By 2023–2025, many months exhibit male and female employment rates of 95–97 percent, with unemployment at 3–5 percent for both. The key driver of gender inequality is therefore who makes it into the labor force at all, not systematic differences in job-finding among those already participating.

Underemployment shows job quality risks for both sexes

Underemployment remains higher than unemployment for both men and women across 2021–2026. Both gradually improve, with many later months showing values closer to 11–13 percent for men and 8–12 percent for women. Both sexes are far more likely to be underemployed than unemployed, with underemployment often running at double or more the corresponding unemployment rate. That is the job-quality fault line AI will interact with.

Sector shifts: from fields to services

Agricultural employment steadily declines from about 32 percent in 2012 to roughly 18 percent by 2025–2026. Industry stabilizes near 18 percent. Services do the heavy lifting, increasing from about 53 percent of employment in 2012 to around 64 percent in recent years.

This means that by the time generative AI enters the conversation, a growing majority of Filipino workers are already in services: trade, BPO, clerical and administrative work, and other urban service activities. Global research identifies many of these occupations as relatively exposed to AI-enabled automation and task restructuring, far more than traditional agriculture or many forms of manual industry.

So what does all this mean for AI and jobs?

No visible macro shock—yet

When overlaying the AI window on monthly data, the most striking feature is the absence of a break. Employment is already improving before generative AI becomes mainstream, and the decline in unemployment from around 7–9 percent to about 3–5 percent continues smoothly. At the national level, it is hard to argue that AI has already produced a visible macro shock to employment or unemployment. The data suggest current improvements are driven primarily by post-COVID recovery and longer-run structural dynamics.

Underemployment and the risk of “not enough work”

Underemployment remains the most important structural warning sign. Nationally, it falls from roughly 20–21 percent in the mid-2000s to around 11–13 percent by the mid-2020s, but never approaches the low single digits seen in unemployment. The central risk is not just job loss, but job quality. As AI reshapes tasks in services and clerical work, it could upgrade jobs—raising productivity, wages, and hours. But it could also deepen precarious arrangements, where workers remain technically employed but underutilized and underpaid.

Where this leaves the AI and jobs debate

The Philippine labor market enters the AI era from a position of partial strength and partial fragility. Employment is high, unemployment is lower than in the mid-2000s, and the economy has largely recovered from COVID-19. On the downside, underemployment remains structurally elevated, gender gaps in participation persist, and the bulk of employment has shifted into service sectors more exposed to AI-enabled change.

Rather than treating AI as an immediate macro shock, it is more accurate to see current data as a baseline for future AI-era change—one shaped by participation gaps, service-sector expansion, and high underemployment.

The AI conversation in the Philippines should stay cautious, empirical, and distribution-aware: less about whether “AI kills jobs” in the abstract, and more about which workers, in which sectors, see their work transformed—and on what terms.
